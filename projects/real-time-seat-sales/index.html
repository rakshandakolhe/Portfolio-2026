Real-Time Seat Sales: Designing high-stakes B2B purchasing for inventory that disappears in minutes

Converted a stakeholder-focused platform into a transactional purchasing experience, creating a complete end-to-end journey for corporate clients to secure Rugby World Cup 2027 inventory in real time.

Lead Designer

Web

The Problem

Corporate clients had no way to purchase Rugby World Cup 2027 seat inventory directly — the platform was built for stakeholder visibility, not transactional commerce. B2B buyers were forced through manual processes, causing delays, errors, and lost sales during time-critical inventory windows.

01 Context & Stakes

Why this mattered to the business

The B2B seat sales flow handled purchases from account managers, corporate clients, and group booking coordinators who were allocating large blocks of seats across live sports and entertainment events. Unlike consumer ticketing, these buyers were responsible for pre-selling those seats to their own clients — meaning a failed, slow, or error-prone purchase had downstream commercial consequences.

Platform Overview

CTP (Client Ticketing Platform) is Ticketmaster's business-to-business ticketing platform, used by sponsors, corporate partners, and participating rugby unions to manage ticket allocations for major tournaments. Unlike consumer ticketing platforms designed for fans, CTP supports organizations purchasing tickets on behalf of clients, guests, and hospitality programs.

The Operational Reality

During live tournaments, ticket availability is highly dynamic. Stadium reconfigurations, returned allocations, and reclaimed sponsor inventory can release new seats at any time—often just hours before an event. These releases frequently involve high-demand matches and limited availability.

The Existing Experience

At the time, CTP operated on a Request → Approval model. Users could request available inventory, but purchases were not completed in real time. Instead, requests were reviewed and approved manually before tickets could be secured. While effective for planned allocation management, the workflow was not designed for inventory releases where availability could change within minutes.

The Opportunity

For Rugby World Cup 2027, Ticketmaster wanted to enable eligible stakeholders to purchase approved inventory directly within the platform, reducing operational overhead while creating a faster and more transparent purchasing experience. Achieving this required transforming a platform built for allocation management into a transactional purchasing experience supporting inventory discovery, seat selection, basket management, and checkout.

02 Problem Definition

What was broken, and why it mattered

Before designing anything new, a heuristic and accessibility audit was conducted across the existing CTP experience. The goal was to surface the violations already present in the platform — issues that would follow us into the new purchasing flow if left unresolved. What emerged wasn't a list of cosmetic issues. Each finding mapped to a real failure mode in a high-stakes purchasing context.

1. The approval model is incompatible with real-time inventory

When inventory drops unpredictably, the Request→Approval flow introduces too much latency. By the time approval completes, high-demand seats are gone. There is no mechanism for eligible stakeholders to act immediately.

2. No transactional infrastructure exists on CTP

CTP generates a "request entity" for payments — not an order entity. Basket, payment, and confirmation flows are net-new surfaces with no pattern to extend. Every design decision either creates a scalable foundation or a fragile workaround. This was not in the original brief — it surfaced through platform analysis.

3. Dual-mode stakeholder management creates collision risk

Non-union corporates (RTSS) and participating unions (Request→Approval) operate differently. Nothing in the current design explicitly differentiates these flows — a corporate stakeholder could land in the wrong journey entirely.

4. Platform decisions need to scale beyond RWC

Market segments, CRC seat pools, and workflow routing are all configurable systems. Design decisions made for RWC 2027 will either scale across the platform or create tournament-specific debt for every subsequent client.

Scope & Constraints

To meet the Rugby World Cup 2027 delivery timeline, the MVP focused on enabling a complete end-to-end purchasing journey for eligible stakeholders. Several adjacent capabilities were intentionally deferred, handled by existing systems, or owned by separate teams to keep the project focused on the highest-impact user and business outcomes.

MVP Constraints

  • •Interactive Seat Map — Planned as a separate workstream with an independent delivery timeline.
  • •Resale & Ticket Management — Reserved for a future phase beyond the initial purchasing experience.
  • •Backoffice Configuration — Managed by a separate product team and excluded from the design scope.
  • •Invoice Generation — Existing invoicing infrastructure remained unchanged; the scope focused on invoice access and triggering within the purchasing flow.
  • •Ticket Holder Assignment (PSA) — Scheduled for a subsequent phase following the MVP launch.
  • •Entitlements Management — Deferred to a later release; the MVP relied on predefined purchase limits rather than entitlement-based controls.

03 Users & Context

Who we were designing for

Designing for corporate procurement introduced a fundamentally different mental model from consumer ticketing. The challenge was not only to support distinct purchasing behaviors, but also to create a shared platform experience that could serve multiple stakeholder groups with clarity and confidence.

The Corporate Buyer

RTSS Purchase Flow · Sponsors, Commercial Partners, Key Accounts

A goal-driven user focused on securing specific inventory under time pressure. Unlike consumer ticket buyers, they operate within procurement workflows, often purchasing in volume and requiring clear visibility into allocation limits, purchasing constraints, and invoice-based fulfillment processes.

The Union Representative

Request → Approval Flow · Participating Unions

Operates within an established allocation and approval process. As this workflow remained unchanged, the primary design consideration was maintaining clear separation from the RTSS purchasing experience and ensuring users were automatically routed to the correct flow upon login.

04 Discovery & Research

Design Principles & Constraints

Evaluating the Foundation

Before introducing new capabilities, I assessed the existing ticket selection experience against usability and accessibility standards to understand where the current design was creating friction and where foundational issues needed to be addressed.

Understanding the Platform Architecture

Before exploring interface solutions, I mapped the underlying platform architecture to understand how inventory, entitlement rules, ticket states, and financial systems interacted across CTP. This revealed that RTSS was fundamentally a platform integration challenge rather than a standalone workflow. By identifying upstream dependencies and downstream impacts early, the resulting solution could be designed as a reusable platform capability rather than a Rugby World Cup–specific implementation.

Designing for a Different Mental Model

A critical insight emerged early in the project: corporate procurement behaviour differs fundamentally from consumer ticket purchasing. Unlike fans who browse, compare, and explore options, corporate buyers typically arrive with a defined inventory objective and operate under strict time constraints.

This meant many familiar consumer-ticketing patterns were not only unnecessary but actively detrimental to task completion. The experience needed to prioritize operational efficiency through clear purchasing constraints, reduced decision friction, and procurement-focused post-purchase workflows.

Recognizing this distinction established a core design principle: optimize for execution, not discovery.

"When inventory becomes available, speed matters more than discovery. I need to understand my limits, make a decision, and secure the seats before someone else does."
"Unlike a fan purchase, this is part of a procurement process. The confirmation is useful, but the invoice is what allows me to complete the transaction internally."

Constraints That Shaped the Design

Before exploring solutions, it was important to understand the platform, business, and operational constraints that would shape the final experience.

Platform Constraints

CTP-Native Experience — The purchasing journey needed to be built entirely within CTP. A planned handoff to an existing Ticketmaster transactional surface was ruled out, requiring every stage — from inventory selection through confirmation — to be designed within the platform.

No Existing Order Model — CTP was built around request management rather than transactional purchasing. The platform did not contain a dedicated order entity, requiring post-purchase and confirmation states to be designed around a future B2B order model rather than existing request-based patterns.

Business Constraints

Configurable Purchase Limits — Purchase limits vary by client segment and are managed through back-office configuration. The experience could not rely on fixed Rugby World Cup values and needed to support changing entitlement rules without design changes.

Dual-Mode Platform — The platform needed to support both RTSS purchasing and the existing Request → Approval workflow. Preventing confusion between these operating models was critical, making workflow separation a core design requirement rather than a routing consideration.

Invoice-Driven Procurement — For corporate buyers, the transaction does not end at confirmation. Invoice access is a primary post-purchase action and needed to be treated as a first-class outcome alongside ticket fulfillment.

Experience Constraints

Basket as an Inventory Checkpoint — Unlike traditional ecommerce baskets, inventory remained highly dynamic throughout the transaction. The basket therefore functioned as an active validation checkpoint, continuously communicating availability, purchase limits, and purchasing constraints before checkout.

05 Design Principles

The Three Objectives the Solution Had to Satisfy

Before exploring solutions, I defined three success criteria that would guide the project. Every concept, interaction, and design decision was measured against these principles to ensure the experience remained focused on the core user and business objectives.

01 · Leveraging Existing Allocation Technology
Rather than creating a new seat allocation system, the solution built upon an existing Best Available capability already used elsewhere in the business.

The challenge was adapting the technology to support a different customer segment, purchasing model, and platform requirements while maintaining a familiar and scalable allocation experience.

02 · Operational Efficiency
Every interaction should move the buyer closer to securing inventory.

This experience was designed for task completion, not exploration. Buyers arrive with a clear objective and limited time, making unnecessary decisions, navigation, or promotional patterns a source of friction rather than value.

03 · Platform Scalability
The solution must scale beyond a single tournament.

Business rules, purchasing constraints, payment methods, and system states should be driven by configuration rather than event-specific logic. Designing for flexibility ensures the platform can support future tournaments, clients, and operational models without requiring fundamental redesign.

06 Explorations

Exploration & Trade-Offs

Embedded Checkout Summary

Operational Efficiency

Why it was explored

This direction introduced a persistent order summary alongside category selection, allowing buyers to see pricing, basket value, and selected inventory without leaving the page.

Why it wasn't selected

While the persistent summary improved visibility into purchase progress, it introduced unnecessary cognitive load during inventory selection. Buyers were required to process inventory discovery, category decisions, pricing, and checkout information simultaneously. For corporate buyers operating under FCFS conditions, the primary task is securing inventory as quickly as possible. Surfacing checkout concerns too early competed with the core decision-making process and increased visual complexity without meaningfully improving task completion.

Seat Map-Led Selection

Platform Scalability

Why it was explored

This direction centred the seat map as the primary interaction surface, allowing buyers to select inventory directly from a visual representation of the venue before entering the checkout flow.

Why it wasn't selected

Best Available allocation was designed to optimize speed, fairness, and inventory distribution at scale. Introducing a seat-map-led workflow added decision overhead without improving purchasing outcomes. The final solution focused on category-based selection, allowing users to secure inventory quickly while the allocation engine handled seat assignment behind the scenes.

07 Final Solution

Design decisions, traced to user needs

Design choices were informed by research, not personal preference. Every interaction, pattern, and prioritization was rooted in observed user behaviour and platform requirements.
