Built Scalable Multi-Tournament Component Architecture

Every new tournament format introduced additional design and engineering overhead. Components were recreated, patterns diverged, and delivery slowed as the platform scaled. This project established a flexible component architecture that could accommodate structural differences across tournament types, enabling teams to configure experiences rather than rebuild them.

Lead Designer
Systems Design · Component Architecture · Design Tokens
Web

The Issue

The platform was responsible for ticketing across all tournaments — yet each experience was built independently, with no shared component system. This created inconsistent user experiences, duplicated design and engineering effort, and fragmented behavior across products. Slower rollout timelines and compounding maintenance overhead meant the team couldn't scale new tournaments efficiently, directly threatening their ability to launch on time and at quality.

04 Design Principles

Core Experience Principles

Every design decision — from card hierarchy to accessibility behavior — was evaluated against three foundational principles.

01
Clarity at a Glance
Fans should understand an event card instantly.

Sport, venue, date, and ticket availability were designed to be immediately scannable without requiring cognitive effort or secondary interaction.

02
One System Across All Sports
A single scalable architecture instead of tournament-specific solutions.

The same reusable card and filter system needed to support everything from high-volume Athletics schedules to premium single-session ceremonies without redesigning components per sport.

03
Accessibility by Default
The experience needed to work seamlessly across devices and assistive technologies.

Layouts, hierarchy, and interactions were designed to support screen readers, responsive scaling, keyboard navigation, and clear information comprehension across accessibility needs.

05 Strategy

Defining the experience strategy

The listing experience was designed around two distinct fan behaviours with fundamentally different browsing and decision-making patterns.

🏅
The Sport Fan
Returning user · Knows exactly what they want
  • Arrives with a specific sport and session in mind — filtering is their primary action
  • Needs date, venue, and availability confirmed fast before committing
  • Frustrated by unnecessary friction
🎟️
The Experience Seeker
New or casual user · Browsing for value
  • Doesn't have a sport preference — wants to know what's available and affordable
  • Needs enough context from the card to decide without clicking through
  • Likely comparing multiple events simultaneously across the listing

Reflection & What Changed

This project shifted tournament experiences from isolated implementations toward a scalable platform mindset. Instead of repeatedly rebuilding tournament-specific experiences, the work established reusable foundations capable of supporting future sports events more efficiently and consistently.

The project also strengthened cross-functional collaboration between design and engineering by introducing more scalable workflows, reusable component thinking, and structured implementation alignment.
